Starting from infancy, we are inundated by information from different sources. How do we choose which information to attend to and which source to trust? How do we interpret the information we receive? How do we revise what we believe? How do we justify our beliefs? What kind of biases do we show when seeking and using information? Are there cross-cultural differences in the development of social learning and reasoning? These questions constitute the focus of my previous and current research, whose overarching theme is the development of human social learning and reasoning. To answer these questions and reveal socio-cognitive and developmental mechanisms underlying human learning and reasoning, I conduct research with participants ranging from 3 to 55 years old with diverse cultural and linguistic backgrounds (from Turkey, the UK, and Canada).

How do young children selectively learn from others?

To evaluate informants’ credibility, preschoolers use epistemic and social cues (Tong et al., 2020). Although previous studies showed that children selectively trust informants who provide accurate and valid explanations (Koenig, 2012; Corriveau & Kurkul, 2014), we did not know whether children prefer learning from informants who offer explanations that align with their expectations. Inspired by the notion of the brain as a prediction machine (Clark, 2013), in my master’s thesis, I examined whether and how 5- and 6-year-old children (N=104) use the match or mismatch between their prediction about how an informant would explain a topic and an informant’s actual explanation to decide whom to learn from. We found that 1) young children favored the informants who provided explanations in line with children’s own expectations, and 2) they discounted relevant information coming from informants who did not match their predictions. While this tendency creates alignment in the learning context, it might also make children erroneously discount good quality information coming from individuals who explain differently than themselves.

Cross-linguistic differences in preschoolers’ social learning and reasoning

Preschoolers trust first-hand information over hearsay (Köymen & Tomasello, 2018). Yet, most of the selective trust studies are conducted with English-speaking children. However, languages differ, with some explicitly marking the reliability of the information. For instance, in Turkish, it is grammatically obligatory to express how information was acquired (whether the speaker is reporting direct observation or hearsay), while it is not obligatory in English. We investigated whether Turkish-speaking children might be more attentive and display an earlier sensitivity to such information when deciding whom to believe than English-speaking children. In two projects (a collaboration between Koç University, Turkey, the University of Manchester, UK, and the University of Toronto, Canada), we examined how 3- and 5-year-old Turkish-speaking and English-speaking children (N=290) talk about the reliability of the information they received to convince someone, i.e., metatalk (Özkan et al., 2023) and 2) revise their existing beliefs depending on the reliability of the counterevidence. We found that children as young as three years of age 1) produced metatalk to justify their beliefs and, 2) revised their own beliefs based on the reliability of the information offered to them, 3) Although Turkish-speaking children had advantages in justifying their beliefs, both linguistic groups showed equal performance in revising their beliefs.

How do children and adults interpret others’ testimony?

Most studies of selective social learning target learners’ inferences about the claims of a “generic” individual. Yet, we sometimes need to make speaker-specific inferences using a person’s history to make sense of uttered information. We examined whether and how 5-year-olds, 7-year-olds, and adults (N=163) make inferences about the preferences of informants who always provide good/bad/neutral comments. We found that starting from seven years of age, children can infer that a neutral expression (“It’s okay”) might indicate different levels of liking for someone who always provides positive comments (“It’s good”) versus who always provides negative comments (“It’s bad”). This finding highlights an additional step in selective social learning, the calibration we make using an informant’s history when interpreting evaluative information—an ability that develops with increasing age. Ongoing work is probing younger children’s ability to make such inferences.

Biases in information seeking across the lifespan

We tend to seek, use, and remember more information in favor of our perspective than those against it, i.e., confirmation/myside bias (Nickerson, 1998; Mercier, 2022). This bias leads to overconfidence and polarization (Mercier, 2022). According to the Argumentative Theory of Reasoning, we should see this bias when producing arguments to convince someone (Mercier & Sperber, 2011). Are we also biased before producing arguments: when searching for information? We recently started a preregistered study investigating the biases in information seeking across the lifespan (5, 8, 11, 14, 18-24, 25-55-year-olds, Ntarget = 480) depending on the aim of the information search and the presence of a conflicting perspective. To our knowledge, this study will be the first to offer a comprehensive developmental picture of the biases we show when searching for information for different aims using a novel and same paradigm across the lifespan.
